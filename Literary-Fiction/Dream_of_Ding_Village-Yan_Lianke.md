# Dream of Ding Village - Yan Lianke, Cindy Carter (Translator)

## Summary
Ding Village experiences a dire situation when more than forty villagers die in a short period of time. Most of the villagers develop fevers after selling their blood and later die. The rapid increase of deaths overwhelms the coffinmaker and the maker of the ritual money, causing them to work with blistered hands and no more trees to make coffins. As the number of deaths and villagers with fever grows, the villagers have stopped working with daily chores while waiting for their fate. 

The story has a great background setting, introducing the gloomy scene of the Ding village and the catastrophic experience of the villagers caused by selling blood. The narrator, a twelve-year-old kid who had passed away, reveals that his death is not related to the fever but is connected to his father's act, which led to his death through food poisoning. The story gradually reveals that what the villagers are experiencing is AIDS infection. The narrator also introduces his father, who is a blood merchant, the key character who caused the AIDS epidemic in the village, and also his grandfather, a highly respected elder who acts as a moral compass in the village.

The narrator recounts how the blood selling started in the village, which initially received a cold response from the villagers. The blood selling became a boom after they witnessed the prosperity of a village nearby. The county director who was responsible for the blood selling of several villages got wealthier. Due to the infrequency of the blood collection from the mobile unit, the narrator's father turned the blood selling into a business opportunity. He set up his own private blood bank and resold the blood collection at a higher price. The consequences of the greed only began to manifest ten years later when the villagers experienced fever and death became commonplace.

The story continues with how the AIDS-infected villagers start a commune in the school. Despite most of the villagers knowing their deaths are imminent, it doesn't stop them from experiencing moral distress such as theft, adultery, cheating on food donations, and even power grabs. The narrator's father continues with other money-earning schemes, such as selling government-offered coffins to other villages and matchmaking for the deceased. Simultaneously, the narrator's grandfather tries to make things right on behalf of his two sons, but as the situation is getting worse, he becomes helpless and witnesses the destruction of the village caused by the greed of his elder son.

## Personal Thoughts
- The adult version of Lord of the Flies.
- The writer brilliantly highlights the fragility of human morality, which eventually leads to the falling of the commune. The grandfather begins a commune to take care of the sick villagers and to make amends for his corrupted son. Unfortunately, the villagers who don't share the same moral values as him begin to challenge his integrity.
- The grandfather is a pitiful character, but it's not only because of his two sons' behaviours. The grandfather has his problems with his family to deal with. Additionally, it's almost every day that there are different villagers who ask him for a favour while also threatening him due to this son's misdeeds.
